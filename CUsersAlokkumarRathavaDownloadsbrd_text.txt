3. The Solution — SmartOPD
SmartOPD is a single connected system across eight clinical stations. Every station shares the same patient identity thread — a face scan or an OTP is all a patient, doctor, nurse, or pharmacist ever needs. No ABHA card. No paper file. No physical prescription.

3.2 Module 1 — Entry Kiosk and Face Identification
Face recognition is commodity technology in 2025. The kiosk requires only a standard USB webcam and a mini PC (total hardware cost ₹28,000–₹45,000). Two software approaches are available:
On-premise (recommended): InsightFace open-source library running locally. Face embeddings stored in PostgreSQL with pgvector extension. Raw photos never stored — only the mathematical face vector.
Cloud API option: AWS Rekognition or Azure Face API for hospitals preferring managed services. ~₹0.07 per API call at 300 visits/day = approximately ₹630/month.
DPDP Act 2023 compliance: Face biometric enrolment is opt-in at registration, not automatic at entry. The patient sees a clear notice explaining purpose, gives written consent, and can request deletion at any time. Only the face embedding (not the raw photo) is stored, encrypted at rest.
3.3 Module 2 — Reception and ABDM Health Records
The 'no files' promise is delivered by ABDM (Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission) integration across three milestones:
M1 — ABHA (Ayushman Bharat Health Account): Patient's digital health identity. Created via Aadhaar OTP at first registration. Requires RSA/ECB/PKCS1Padding encryption for sensitive fields.
M2 — Record Linking (KYC_AND_LINK): Every OPD visit creates a care context linked to the patient's ABHA number. This creates the KYC-verified linkages that earn DHIS incentives.
M3 — HIU Consent and Data Pull (ECDH encryption): Patient gives one-time consent for SmartOPD to act as a Health Information User (HIU). From that point, records from every ABDM-connected provider in India appear at reception.
Important limitation: Cross-provider records only appear for providers who are themselves ABDM-connected. Coverage is growing rapidly in urban India but remains uneven in rural and smaller clinic settings. This should be communicated accurately in sales conversations.

3.6 Module 6 — Pharmacy: Paperless Dispensing
The pharmacy module eliminates the most common point of failure in the OPD chain — the paper prescription slip.
Prescription auto-arrives: the moment the doctor signs, the FHIR MedicationRequest appears in the pharmacy queue. No paper, no walking, no lost slips.
Patient identity: face scan in 2 seconds for enrolled patients. For family members collecting on behalf of the patient, an OTP is sent to the patient's registered mobile number — the family member shows the OTP and the system verifies it.
Allergy safety check: pharmacist cannot dispense a drug the patient is allergic to without an explicit clinical override recorded in the audit log.
Dispense log: every drug dispensed is recorded with time, quantity, batch number, and pharmacist ID. This record is appended to the patient's ABDM medication history.
Schedule H and H1 drugs: for the pilot phase, position the pharmacy as an in-house facility dispensing on the attending doctor's instruction. This sidesteps the standalone pharmacy license requirement for digital prescription acceptance while the regulatory framework for ABDM-linked digital prescriptions matures.

4.2 Offline Resilience
Indian hospital networks experience WAN interruptions. The system handles this with three mechanisms:
Local queue: all edge events are written to a disk-backed local queue before being forwarded to the cloud. Events are time-ordered and preserved through power outages.
Idempotent event IDs: every event carries a globally unique ID so that late arrivals are not duplicated when connectivity is restored.
Offline-capable frontends: critical screens (nurse vitals entry, basic prescription issuance) are available via Service Worker when the cloud is unreachable. Data syncs automatically on reconnection.
5. Business Model
SmartOPD has five distinct revenue streams. Most hospital SaaS companies have one — the subscription. The multi-stream architecture means revenue grows faster than the customer base, and one stream alone (DHIS incentive income) can exceed total SaaS revenue at scale.
5.1 Five Revenue Streams
01
SaaS Subscription  —  ₹8,000–₹25,000 per hospital per month
Recurring monthly fee tiered by OPD visit volume and modules enabled. Predictable, contractual, and the metric investors use to price the business. This is the revenue foundation — it funds operations and signals long-term customer commitment.
02
DHIS / DSC Incentive Income  —  ₹5 per KYC-verified linkage (above 100/month)
The government pays hospitals ₹20 per KYC-verified ABDM record linkage (above 100/month). SmartOPD, as the DSC (Digital Solution Company), earns ₹5 per linkage above 100 — once it has 10 active live facilities. This income has near-zero COGS and compounds dramatically at scale: at 50 hospitals it reaches ₹14.4L/month, exceeding total subscription revenue. Unlocks at the 10-facility threshold.
03
Transaction Fees  —  0.5–1% of gross transaction value
Every UPI payment, insurance claim, and lab order that flows through SmartOPD earns a basis-point fee. At 300 visits/day with a ₹400 average consultation fee, the monthly GMV is approximately ₹36L. A 0.7% blended rate generates ₹25,200/month per hospital — adding up to a significant income stream at 50+ hospitals.
04
Equipment Lease Platform Fee  —  ₹50–₹150 per lease originated
SmartOPD manages the complete equipment lease lifecycle: issue, QR tracking, reminders, condition assessment, and deposit settlement. A per-lease platform fee is charged on every lease originated. At 12 active leases per hospital per month this adds ₹600–₹1,800/month per hospital. Phase 3 unlocks home equipment rental referrals, creating a higher-value recurring lease stream.
05
Marketplace Commissions  —  5–12% on routed orders
Lab networks (SRL, Thyrocare, Dr. Lal) pay referral commissions for lab orders routed through SmartOPD. Insurance companies pay lead-generation fees for new policy conversions. Equipment suppliers pay listing fees in Phase 3+. Each partnership forecloses part of the addressable market for competitors — making this both a revenue stream and a moat-building activity.

7. Go-to-Market Strategy
Three sales motions operate in parallel. Each builds on the one before. The incentive-led pilot is the entry point; within-hospital expansion drives net revenue retention above 100%; channel partnerships scale acquisition without proportional sales headcount growth.

Target: first 5 hospitals. Buyer: CMO, Medical Director, or Hospital Administrator.
Lead with the DHIS ROI calculator. Show the decision-maker that SmartOPD generates ₹1L+ per month in new government income. The software pays for itself from day one.
Offer a 60-day pilot: charge only a non-waivable site audit and hardware lease fee (₹40,000–₹80,000). No SaaS fee during the pilot.
First DHIS payout converts the trial. The decision-maker sees real government money deposited into the hospital's account as a direct result of SmartOPD. Annual contract signed immediately after.
SaaS fee begins from month 3. The hospital is already cash-flow positive from DHIS incentives before paying a single rupee in subscription.

Target: department heads, head nurses, pharmacy managers. Goal: NRR above 115%.
Start with Starter plan (entry + reception + pharmacy). Prove value in 30 days with reduced registration time and first DHIS transactions.
Nurse and doctor modules are sold to department heads, not the CMO. The clinical upsell track operates independently of the hospital administration relationship.
Equipment and CRM modules added 3–6 months into the contract when the operational data starts revealing the value (lapsed patients, missed follow-ups, idle equipment).
Each module addition drives net revenue retention above 100%. The average ARPU grows from ₹8,000 (Starter) to ₹28,000+ (Scale + add-ons) over 18 months.

8. Competitive Moat — Why This Is Hard to Copy
Technology can be copied. These four moats cannot — at least not quickly, and not without incurring costs that are prohibitive for most competitors.
8.1 ABDM Certification Lock-in
Strength: Very Strong (5/5)
Getting a system certified to ABDM M1 + M2 + M3 takes 4–8 months of integration work, NHA sandbox testing, security audits, and formal approval. Any competitor must repeat this from scratch. Once hospitals are live on SmartOPD, switching means the hospital loses all DHIS income during the migration gap — a loss of ₹1L+ per month that no hospital administrator will accept. The certification creates a switching cost that is both financial and regulatory.
8.2 Clinical Workflow Stickiness
Strength: Very Strong (5/5)
Once doctors write prescriptions on SmartOPD and nurses log medication administration records on it, the system becomes the authoritative record of care. Switching means: retraining all clinical staff (20–60 users per hospital), migrating 18+ months of FHIR records, re-integrating every pharmacy workflow, and accepting a gap in the patient's ABDM timeline that would concern any regulator. The per-user switching cost in a hospital is dramatically higher than in a typical B2B SaaS setting because clinical staff are time-constrained and risk-averse.
8.3 Clinical Data Network Effect
Strength: Strong (4/5)
Every hospital that joins SmartOPD enriches the ABDM records network for every other hospital's patients. A patient who visits Hospital A and Hospital B — both on SmartOPD — gives both doctors complete cross-hospital history seamlessly, because SmartOPD's ABDM integration handles the HIU consent flow in both directions. The more hospitals on the platform, the more valuable the cross-provider history becomes for each individual hospital. This is a classic two-sided network effect applied to healthcare data.
8.4 Lab and Insurer Partnership Exclusivity
Strength: Medium to Strong (3/5, growing over time)
Once SRL or Thyrocare signs a preferred-routing agreement with SmartOPD, they have reduced incentive to build equally deep integrations with competitors — SmartOPD hospitals already cover their order flow. The same logic applies to insurance TPAs and NHCX claim partnerships. Each partnership forecloses part of the addressable market for the next entrant, and these deals compound over time into a distribution moat that pure software cannot replicate without the same relationships.

Why SmartOPD, Why Now
Three converging forces make 2025–2026 the right moment for SmartOPD:
ABDM has reached critical operational maturity. M1 through M3 are production-ready, the sandbox is well-documented, and NHA is actively incentivising adoption through DHIS. This window — where incentives are live and competition is nascent — will not stay open.

No direct competitor exists. HealthPlix, Practo, and similar products address parts of the problem. None combines face-based biometric identity, ABDM M3 HIU compliance, pharmacy integration, equipment leasing, CRM, and the DSC revenue stream in a single product designed specifically for the Indian mid-tier hospital market.
SmartOPD is not asking hospitals to adopt technology. It is offering to generate ₹1.5L+ per month in new income while simultaneously eliminating the paper chaos that frustrates every clinical staff member. That is a product that sells itself — if the ABDM integration is production-quality and the doctor screen takes under 90 seconds. Those are the two non-negotiable engineering priorities.
